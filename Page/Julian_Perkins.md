JULIAN PERKINS is a British conductor and keyboard player (harpsichord, fortepiano and clavichord).[1] He is Founder Director of the early music ensemble Sounds Baroque and Artistic Director of Cambridge Handel Opera Company.[2]

Julian Perkins has been praised for his 'heartening dramatic energy' and 'dynamic direction',[3][4] while his harpsichord accompaniment has been described as 'superbly creative' and 'wonderfully adept and stylish'.[5][6] His solo discography includes premi√®res of harpsichord suites by James Nares and John Christopher Smith,[7][8] and clavichord works by Herbert Howells and Stephen Dodgson.[9][10] He directed the first recording of Daniel Purcell's opera-oratorio _The Judgment of Paris_,[11] which he subsequently performed in London at St John's, Smith Square.[12] Other recordings include Johann Sebastian Bach's _French Suites_ on clavichord,[13] and Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart's six sonatas for keyboard duet with Emma Abbate.[14][15] With Sounds Baroque, he has made two recordings of Italian cantatas and one of songs by Henry Purcell with the soprano Anna Dennis.[16][17]

In 2009, Julian Perkins performed and recorded solo harpsichord at the Royal Opera House. In 2013, Julian Perkins worked with the Academy of Ancient Music performing in the exhibition about Johannes Vermeer at London's National Gallery.[18] Julian Perkins has conducted staged opera productions for organisations including the Buxton Festival,[19] Dutch National Opera Academy, Grimeborn Festival and Guildhall School of Music & Drama,[20][21] and devised an opera pasticcio about Casanova with librettist Stephen Pettitt that he directed for the _Baroque Unwrapped_ series at London's Kings Place in May 2016.[22] He has appeared live on BBC Radio 3,[23] featured on _The Early Music Show_ and contributed to the station's pioneering podcast about George Frideric Handel's _Orlando_ with Welsh National Opera.[24][25] Concert performances have included appearances as music director for Coram with Simon Callow,[26] Peter Capaldi, Rebecca Evans,[27] Dame Emma Kirkby,[28] Mark Padmore and David Wilson Johnson,[29][30] and solo recitals in international festivals at St Albans, Buxton, Canterbury, Deal, Lammermuir, Northern Aldborough, Oundle, Petworth, Roman River, Ryedale, Swaledale, Two Moors and Tel Aviv. He wrote an introduction to the clavichord for _BBC Music Magazine_,[31] and his article about John Christopher Smith was published by _Early Music Today_.[32] He is a harpsichord tutor at the Royal Northern College of Music.[33]
