KUMIKO TANAKA-ISHII (born 1969) is a computational linguist and an Associate Professor in the Department of Creative Informatics at the Graduate School of Information Science and Technology at the University of Tokyo, Japan.[1] She is the author of _Semiotics of Programming_, an award-winning book semiotically analyzing computer programs along three axes: models of signs, kinds of signs, and systems of signs.[2]


Personal

Tanaka-Ishii received her doctorate from the University of Tokyo in 1997. In 1995, before completing her PhD, she was a visiting researcher at Laboratoire d'Informatique pour la Mécanique et les Sciences de l'Ingénieur (LIMSI) at the National Center for Scientific Research (CNRS) in Paris, where she worked on semantic proximity matrices for the Japanese language.[3] In 2010 she was awarded both the Suntory Prize for Social Sciences and Humanities and the Okawa Publications Prize for her book, _Semiotics of Programming_. The book has been critically and favorably reviewed in _Linguistic & Philosophical Investigations_,[4] _Cognitive Technology Journal_,[5] and _Semiotica_.[6]
