ÉMILE SERVAN-SCHREIBER (1888-1967) was a French journalist. He was the co-founder of _Les Échos_. He was the author of several books.


Early life

Émile Servan-Schreiber was born as Émile Schreiber on December 20, 1888 in Paris, France.[1] His father, Joseph Schreiber, was a Jewish-Prussian immigrant.[2][3] His mother, born Clara Feilchenfeld, spoke Yiddish.[4] His patronym, Schreiber, means "writer" in German.[5] He had three brothers, André, Georges and Robert.[6]

Servan-Schreiber was educated at the Collège Rollin in Paris.[7] During World War I, he served in the French Army.[8] He received the Croix de Guerre for his service.[9]


Career

Servan-Schreiber was a journalist.[10] He co-founded _Les Échos_ with his brother André in 1908.[11] He was also a contributor; for example, in 1960, he wrote that the Algerian War could not be compared to World War I, as more French people were dying in car accidents than on the battlefield.[12] The newspaper was sold by the Schreibers in 1963.[13]

Servan-Schreiber was a journalist for _L'Illustration_.[14] He was the author of several books,[15] beginning with _L'exemple américain_ in 1917, when he called for an Americanisation of France with regards to comfort and cleanliness.[16] His third book, _Rome après Moscou_, was reviewed by George N. Shuster in _Books Abroad_,[17] while his fifth book, _L'Amérique Réagit_, was reviewed by Albert J. Guerard, also in _Books Abroad_.[18] His ninth book, _Le Portugal de Salazar_, was reviewed by Armando Frumento in _Giornale degli Economisti e Annali di Economia_.[19]


Personal life and death

Servan-Schreiber married Denise Brésard, a Roman Catholic.[20] They had two sons, Jean-Jacques Servan-Schreiber and Jean-Louis Servan-Schreiber, and three daughters, Brigitte Gros, Bernadette Gradis, and Christiane Collange.[21]

Prior to World War II, Servan-Schreiber purchased "Chalet Nanouk", a chalet in Mont d'Arbois upon the recommendation of Baroness Noémie de Rothschild.[22] During the war, he took the pseudonym of "Servan" and hid in the chalet with his parents, his wife and his children.[23] Servan-Schreiber legally changed his name on November 5, 1952, adding "Servan" before "Schreiber".[24]

Servan-Schreiber died on December 29, 1967 in Veulettes-sur-Mer, France.[25]
