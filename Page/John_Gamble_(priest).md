JOHN GAMBLE (1762–1811) was a British Anglican clergyman and military chaplain. From 1796 to 1810, he served as the first Chaplain General to the Forces and head of the Army Chaplains' Department.[1][2][3]

Gamble was educated at Pembroke College, Cambridge, graduating B.A. 1784, M.A. 1787, and becoming a Fellow of the college, He was chaplain to the Duke of York and Albany, and became chaplain-general of the forces. He was rector of Alphamstone, and of Bradwell-juxta-mare in Essex. He died at Knightsbridge on 27 July 1811.[4][5] He was minister of Knightsbridge's Trinity Chapel, and had resided there, at 3 South Place, from 1801.[6]

In 1795 Gamble published a pamphlet _Observations on Telegraphic Experiments, or the different Modes which have been or may be adopted for the purpose of Distant Communication_. He followed it with _Essay on the different Modes of Communication by Signals_ (1797).[7] He is credited with the invention at this period of a six-arm semaphore.[8]
