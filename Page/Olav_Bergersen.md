OLAV BERGERSEN (2 July 1880 – 1973) was a Norwegian naval officer, war historian, insurance inspector and politician. He was a member of the Storting 1931–1933, and served with the Admiral Staff of the Royal Norwegian Navy in London during the Second World War.


Personal life

Bergersen was born in Strinda to judge Bernhard Konrad Bergersen and Marie Magdalene Selmer, and was a great-grandson of politician Hilmar Meincke Krohg.[1] He married Ynghild Gønnning in 1906.[2]


Career

Bergersen graduated as naval officer in 1901. He was promoted to Premier Lieutenant in 1904, to Captain in 1912, and Commander in 1945. He was assigned with the insurance company _Trondhjems Forsikringsselskap_ from 1919 to 1945.[3]

He was a member of the municipal council of Horten from 1910 to 1915, and the city council of Trondheim from 1925 to 1937. He was a deputy member of the Storting 1928–1930 (meeting for Worm Hirsch Darre-Jenssen in 1928), and was elected representative to the Storting for the period 1931–1933, for the Conservative Party.[4] During the Second World War he was held five months in 1942 at the Falstad concentration camp,[5] as a hostage. In 1943 he escaped German-occupied Norway to neutral Sweden and made his way to the United Kingdom, where he served with the admiral staff of the Royal Norwegian Navy.[6]

He was member of the advisory board of the Norwegian Maritime Museum and a board member of _Nordenfjeldske Kunstindustrimuseum_.[7]

His books include the two-volume _Viceadmiral Tordenskiold_ (1925), _Tordenskiold og danskene_ (1932),[8] _Fra Henrik Bielke til Iver Huitfeldt_ (1953–56, four volumes),[9] and _Tragedien omkring Tordenskiolds død_ (1963).[10] He edited a book on Tordenskiold's letters in 1964,[11] and wrote the two-volume _Nøytralitet og krig_ in 1966.[12]

He was awarded the Norwegian Defence Medal 1940–1945 and the King's Medal of Merit in gold, and was decorated Commander of the Order of St. Olav in 1962.[13]
