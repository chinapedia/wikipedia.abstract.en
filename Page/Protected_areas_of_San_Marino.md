The Republic of San Marino has no protected areas as of November, 2016.[1] San Marino is defined as a Global 200 Ecoregions.[2] San Marino has no threatened or endangered species.[3]


International law

San Marino accepted the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora, adheres to the International Convention for the Regulation of Whaling, and ratified the World Heritage Convention.[4] They are members of International Maritime Organization and IPCC.[5] They are signatories to the Convention on Biological Diversity.[6]


Domestic law

While San Marino has no official protected areas, it has a law prohibiting the cutting down of any tree over in diameter, meaning that most trees within the country are legally protected.[7] Under the FRA 2010 Categorization of Forests, all of the land area of San Marino is considered "Other land", with none being "Forest" or "Other wooded land".[8] Despite the fact that 16% of the country is woodlands, predominantly of oaks and other broadleaves as _Quercus pubescens_, _Fraxinus ornus_, _Ostrya carpinifolia_, _Acer obtusatum_, _Quercus cerris_, _Quercus ilex_, _Populus nigra_, _Salix alba_. Shrublands and similar lands are dominated by _Crataegus monogyna_, _Cornus sanguinea_, _Spartium junceum_, _Rubus ulmifolius_, _Rosa canina_, _Prunus spinosa_, _Quercus pubescens_, _Fraxinus ornus_ and _Ulmus minor_ cover the 17%. Badlands are only 4% of the country, and less than 1% is covered by rivers.[9]
