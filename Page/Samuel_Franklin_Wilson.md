SAMUEL FRANKLIN WILSON (1845-1923) was an American Confederate veteran, politician and judge.


Early life

Samuel Franklin Wilson was born in April 1845 in Sumner County, Tennessee.[1] He was of English descent.[2] During paternal great-great-uncle, Zachary Wilson, was a signatory of the Mecklenburg Declaration of Independence.[3] His father was Samuel Wilson and his mother, Nancy Moore.[4] He had seven siblings.[5]

During the American Civil War of 1861-1865, he served under Colonel William B. Bate and General Edmund Kirby Smith in the Confederate States Army.[6] He lost an arm at the Battle of Chickamauga, and he was amputated.[7]

After the war, Wilson graduated from the University of Georgia in 1868.[8] He received a law degree from Cumberland University.[9]


Career

Wilson practised the law in Gallatin, Tennessee.[10]

Wilson was a member of the Democratic Party.[11] He served in the Tennessee House of Representatives from 1877 to 1879, sitting on the judiciary committee.[12] He was elected to the Tennessee Senate in 1879, and served as the chairman of its judiciary committee.[13] He was elected by the "low taxers" to represent Tennessee at the 1880 Democratic National Convention, but he lost to Alvin Hawkins.[14]

Wilson was appointed as a United States Marshal from 1885 to 1889, under President Grover Cleveland.[15] He served as a Judge on the Tennessee Court of Chancery Appeals from 1895 to 1901.[16]


Personal life and death

Wilson married Mary Lytton Bostick.[17] They had two sons and three daughters.[18] He died in 1923.
