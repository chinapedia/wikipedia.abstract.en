PEARL WILLIAMS-JONES () (June 28, 1931 â€“ February 4, 1991) was an American gospel musician.

A native of Washington, D.C., Williams was the daughter of Smallwood Edmond Williams, pastor of the Bible Way Church of Our Lord Jesus Christ.[1] She attended public schools in the District, graduating from Charles Young Elementary, Brown Junior High School and Dunbar High School.[2] She studied piano with Hazel Harrison and Natalie Hinderas while attending Howard University, from which she received both a bachelor's degree and a master's of music,[3] and from which she graduated _magna cum laude_.[4] She served as minister of music at her father's church and performed as a singer and pianist throughout the United States and Europe,[5] appearing in such venues as Carnegie Hall, Lincoln Center, the Kennedy Center, Wigmore Hall in London and the Suphiensalle in Munich.[6] A well-regarded scholar of gospel music, she spent decades as a professor of music at the University of the District of Columbia, where she developed the first degree program in the United States dedicated to the study and performance of gospel.[7] She taught jazz history and music appreciation as well, and directed the university's gospel choir.[8] She served as a technical advisory on the film _Say Amen, Somebody_.[9] For two decades she consulted with the Smithsonian Folklife Festival, and worked as an administrative staff member of its African Diaspora Advisory Group.[10] Williams-Jones published a number of works, including a study of the work of Roberta Martin written with Bernice Johnson Reagon.[11] As a composer she was especially known for her performance of "Jesus, Lover of My Soul" to the accompaniment of Johann Sebastian Bach's "Jesu, Joy of Man's Desiring".[12][13][14]

Williams-Jones received an honorary degree from Lynchburg College in 1972.[15] She died in 1991 after an 18-month battle with cancer.[16] She was married to Williams V. Jones, MD, with whom she had two children, Yvonne and Virgil Jr.[17]
