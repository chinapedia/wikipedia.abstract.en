_EUCALYPTUS SHIRLEYI_, commonly known as SHIRLEY'S SILVER LEAFED IRONBARK,[1] is a eucalypt that is native to Queensland.[2]


Description

The small tree typically grows to a height of and usually has a crooked trunk. The bark is dark grey to black in colour usually rough and deeply fissured. Adult leaves are opposite, and bluish in colour with an orbicular or broadly lanceolate shape. The leaves are stalkless, stem-clasping or heart-shaped at the base. The blade is long and wide with new growth a silver to blue-grey colour.[3]

The tree blooms between March and May producing creamy yellow flowers that are about in diameter and appear in clusters of three to seven with several clusters forming panicles. Fruits form later in the form of capsules that are egg-shaped, bluish in colour, usually ribbed, with a length of and a width of and with enclosed valves.[4]


Taxonomy

The species was first formally described by the botanist url=https://bie.ala.org.au/species/http://id.biodiversity.org.au/node/apni/2916605#names}} The specific epithet honours John Shirley, an educationist and scientist, who also served as on the council of the Royal Society of Queensland for 33 years and was twice President.[5]


Distribution

The species is found in eastern parts of North Queensland and southern parts of Far North Queensland with a scattered distribution through coastal areas that are further south.[6]
