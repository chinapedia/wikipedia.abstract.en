JASIRI X (born Jasiri Oronde Smith) is a Pittsburgh-based rapper and activist[1] who gained attention for his 2007 song "Free the Jena 6."[2] He is a recipient of the Rauschenberg Artist as Activist award[3] and founder of anti-violence group 1Hood.[4][5] In 2016, he was awarded an honorary doctorate from the Chicago Theological Seminary.[6]


Early life

Jasiri X was born Jasiri Oronde Smith[7] in Chicago, Illinois. In the 1980s he moved with his mother to Monroeville, Pennsylvania and attended Gateway High School, graduating at age 16. Intending to become a lawyer, he began college at the University of Maryland, then attended the University of Pittsburgh but did not finish college.[8]


Career

After leaving school, Jasiri began spoken word,[9] performing initially as Jo Smith.[10] He also became an activist and was introduced to the Nation of Islam, taken by an acquaintance to a mosque in 1997.[11] Jasiri met Louis Farrakhan a year later when Farrakhan was in Pittsburgh to support a boycott, and Jasiri subsequently registered as a member the Nation of Islam. In 2005, he became the minister of Muhammad Mosque No. 22 in Wilkinsburg.[12]

Jasiri X has created music addressing social and political issues, including his 2007 song "Free the Jena 6",[13] "Trayvon" following George Zimmerman's shooting of Trayvon Martin in February 2012,[14] "Do We Need to Start a Riot?"[15] and "What if the Tea Party was Black?"[16][17] He encouraged other hip-hop artists to engage these concerns as well.[18][19]

In 2010 he released his first album, _American History X_.[20] In 2013, he released a second album, called _Ascension,_ with Vancouver label Wandering Worx_._[21] In 2015, he released _Black Liberation Theology_, with features from David Banner and Tef Poe among others.[22] He has mentioned Nas, Wu-Tang Clan, Mobb Deep, Lauryn Hill, Tupac, the Notorious B.I.G., Public Enemy, and KRS-One as among his musical influences.[23]

Jasiri X also has a YouTube-based show "This Week with Jasiri X"[24] and has been an advocate for net neutrality[25] particularly as it relates to protecting access for low-income people of color.[26]

In 2006, he cofounded the anti-violence group 1Hood,[27] which includes a media academy teaching young black boys to analyze media as well as create their own.[28]

Awards and grants

-   2015 "Artist as Activist" fellowship at the Robert Rauschenberg Foundation[29][30]
-   2015 USA Fellow, a $50,000 award from United States Artists for "innovative, accomplished artists...for their fresh perspectives, unique artistic vision, and impact on their field"[31]
-   2015 commission from the Open Society Foundation to create a film on the effects of the drug war on South America[32]
-   2016 honorary doctorate from the Chicago Theological Seminary[33]
