WILLIAM REDD, also known as SI REDD (1911-2003) was an American businessman and philanthropist. He was the founder of International Game Technology, a slot machine manufacturer and distributor. He was the owner of the Oasis, a hotel and casino in Mesquite, Nevada. He was the rightsholder of video poker, and he became known as the "king of slot machines".[1]


Early life

William Redd was born on November 16, 1911 in Union, Mississippi.[2][3][4] His father was a sharecropper.[5] He grew up in Philadelphia, Mississippi.[6]

Redd attended East Mississippi Junior College in Decatur, Mississippi,[7] and he graduated from the University of Mississippi in Oxford, Mississippi.[8][9]


Career

Redd began his career in college, when he invested in a pinball machine in a small eatery in Mississippi.[10] He subsequently founded Northwestern Music Co., and he distributed Wurlitzer jukeboxes in Sterling, Illinois and Dixon, Illinois with his brother-in-law.[11][12] He subsequently became a distributor for Bally Manufacturing in Boston, Massachusetts.[13] In 1967, he moved on to the Reno, Nevada market.[14] Redd founded a subsidiary, Bally Distribution Co.,[15][16] and he distributed jukeboxes in Carson City, Nevada and Las Vegas.[17] He also acquired the rights to video poker.[18] In 1975, he founded Sircoma,[19] later known as the International Game Technology, a slot machine manufacturer and distributor based in Reno, Nevada.[20] He sold it to Gtech in 1986, and he served on its board of directors until 1991.[21]

Redd developed Pride of Mississippi, a gaming boat off the coast of Mississippi on the Gulf of Mexico,[22] but it went bankrupt and he lost US$20 million in it.[23] He was the owner of Oasis, a hotel and casino in Mesquite, Nevada from 1976 to 2001.[24] He sold it for US$31 million.[25] Meanwhile, he founded the Mesquite Vistas Land Development Co. and the Oasis Golf Course.[26]

Redd was inducted into the Gaming Hall of Fame in 1991,[27][28] and the Nevada Business Hall of Fame in 2002.[29]


Philanthropy

Redd made charitable contributions to the University of Nevada, Las Vegas,[30] where he was a member of the UNLV Gift Club Palladium Society.[31] Moreover, the Si Redd Room and the Redd Vision video screen scoreboard at the Thomas & Mack Center are named in his honor.[32] He received the Chin's Humanitarian of the Year Award from the Muscular Dystrophy Association in 2001.[33]

Redd founded the Las Vegas International Cultural Trade Center and Wild Animal Conservancy.[34] He donated US$150,000 to Problem Gambling Consultants, a non-profit organization for gambling addicts.[35]


Personal life, death and legacy

Redd was married three times. His first wife, Ivy Lee, died in 1974.[36] His second wife, Marilyn, died in 1996.[37] His third wife, Tamara, outlived him.[38] He had two daughters, Vinnie Copeland and Sherry Green.[39] He resided in Las Vegas, Nevada and summered in Solana Beach, California.[40][41] He was a member of the Las Vegas Country Club.[42]

Redd died on October 14, 2003 in Solana Beach, California.[43][44] He was 91 years old.[45][46] His funeral was held at the Palm Mortuary in Las Vegas, Nevada.[47]

His son-in-law, Alan Green, serves as the chief executive of the Mesquite Vistas Land Development Co. and the Oasis Golf Course.[48]
