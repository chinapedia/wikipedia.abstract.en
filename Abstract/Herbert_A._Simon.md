HERBERT ALEXANDER SIMON (June 15, 1916 – February 9, 2001) was an American economist, political scientist and cognitive psychologist, whose primary research interest was decision-making within organizations and is best known for the theories of "bounded rationality" and "satisficing".[1] He received the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1978 and the Turing Award in 1975.[2][3] His research was noted for its interdisciplinary nature and spanned across the fields of cognitive science, computer science, public administration, management, and political science.[4] He was at Carnegie Mellon University for most of his career, from 1949 to 2001.[5]
